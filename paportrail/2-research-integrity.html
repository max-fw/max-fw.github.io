What is research integrity

Guiding principles of research integrity

  • Reliability. Ensuring quality of research in all stages of the research process.
  • Honesty. developing, conducting, sharing, and engaging with research in fair and transparent ways.
  • Respect. for research participants, colleagues, society, culture and the environment.
  • Accountability for the research, its management and its wider impacts.

What is research integrity?

Research integrity involves making honest, responsible decisions at all stages of the research cycle. Planning, data collection, data analysis, writing up, reporting, dissemination.

Why is research integrity important?

  1. It reduces the likelihood of research misconduct.
  2. It ensures validity of research results.
  3. It facilitates trust between researchers.
  4. It promotes trust in data and research expertise.

Why is research integrity important for you?

The research practices that you learn will influence how you conduct and use research now and in the future.

As a research student, you should focus on building your skills. Skills like academic honesty, including learning to site sources completely and accurately, so that you can give credit where credit is due.

Or data interpretation. By learning best practices for data analysis and reporting in your discipline, you can be sure to report your findings accurately.

Research integrity can also help you minimize research waste. By making responsible decisions about whether research should be done and how it should be done, you can minimize waste of time and resources.

  • Wasteful behaviors include conducting unnecessary research on topics that have already been addressed.

  • Not properly designing your study or being careless in your research activities are wasteful behaviors.

  • Conducting research without being transparent about what you did can also be wasteful.

What practices can compromise research integrity?

Plagiarism, including self-plagiarism, giving or taking credit for work inappropriately.

Fabricating data, misrepresenting data, manipulation of images or other materials, undeclared conflicts of interest.

What is plagiarism?

Plagiarism is a practice of misrepresenting someone else‚Äôs words or ideas as your own. It is also possible to self-plagiarize.

This happens when an author passes off old work as new. For example, by submitting one paper for credit in two separate modules.

How do you avoid plagiarism?

  1. Always acknowledge the work and contributions of other people.
  2. When paraphrasing or summarizing someone else‚Äôs work, include a citation.
  3. If including text from someone else‚Äôs work verbatim, use quotation marks and include a citation.
  4. Cite primary sources of information rather than secondary or tertiary sources.

Your university will have resources to help you avoid plagiarism, too. In UCC, for example, a citing, referencing, and plagiarism course is available here.

And check out this video on how to cite sources. And this one on examples of plagiarism. They are short but very useful.

Giving or taking credit for work inappropriately

If someone makes a meaningful contribution to a research project, they should receive credit for their contribution. This might mean they are an author on a final paper or receive an acknowledgement on that paper.

There is guidance to determine if someone makes a meaningful contribution like the IC JME criteria.

You might also ask yourself, did they contribute to the study idea design? Did they conduct analyses or contribute to interpreting findings? Did they assist with writing up the final report paper?

If someone doesn‚Äôt make a meaningful contribution, they shouldn‚Äôt be given credit. For instance, listing uninvolved members of a research group or more senior academics who only looked at the final paper. These kind of authors are sometimes called ghost authors.

Data fabrication

Data fabrication is a process of manufacturing or making up data. For example, an anthropologist might write transcripts of interviews they never conducted. These transcripts, false data, might become part of the research record.

This can result in unreliable data and waste research resources through attempted replications.

Covid 19 example

A paper published in 2020 reported increased risk of debt for people hospitalized with Covid 19 if they had underlying cardiovascular disease.

Findings were based on data supposedly collected by the company of one of the study authors. It quickly became apparent that the data was fraudulent. The paper was retracted, but such questionable practices can undermine trust in research.

Data misrepresentation

Data misrepresentation is a practice of presenting real data in a misleading way. Researchers might emphasize exciting or novel findings only. Researchers might also distort or duplicate images of their findings.

Some researchers even magnified or reverse their images to try and avoid detection.

Data misrepresentation gives an incomplete picture of the research findings. Like other questionable research practices, it can result in unreliable data. It can also waste research resources through attempted replications.

Piracy and manipulation of images, other materials

Sometimes researchers manipulate figures, images, or other resources to include them in multiple research papers. Often these manipulated materials come from organizations known as paper mills.

Paper mills turn out fake data, images, and whole papers to meet demand from researchers. But there are also researchers working to identify this manipulated content so it can be retracted.

Undeclared conflicts of interest

A conflict of interest is when a researcher‚Äôs other interests lead to intentional or unintentional biases in research.

Conflicts of interest may in turn compromise the validity of the results.

For example, a researcher receiving funding for pharmaceutical development may be incentivized to demonstrate that competitors drugs are unsafe or inferior. This may shape how they conduct their research and impact the findings which will influence people‚Äôs health and care in the future.

Why do people engage in research misconduct?

There are several factors that can motivate research misconduct. Some are emotional like fear of failure. Misconduct may also be motivated by external pressures like pressure from supervisors to achieve a certain result.

Researchers may also feel pressure to perform due to the competitiveness of academic culture. Worries about personal circumstances like employment status might also motivate misconduct.

How can you maintain high levels of research integrity?

How can I help reduce the effects of research misconduct?

  1. Follow best practices in research integrity and open research at all stages of the research process.
  2. Avoid plagiarism.
  3. Accurately and honestly represent your data.
  4. Give credit where it is due.
  5. Be transparent and responsible in all aspects of and decisions about your research.

How can I help reduce the effects of research misconduct?

You can also comment on and review already published papers using platforms like Pubpeer. This helps flag issues in research that are already in the public domain.

Pubier is an online journal club that exists as an app on the Chrome browser. It allows researchers to comment on published journal articles. This is called postpublication peer review.

Researchers can highlight problems in analysis, interpretation, and/or reporting of results. Pubier has contributed to many corrections and retractions of published articles.

Most universities have a research code of conduct that outlines what to do if you suspect research misconduct in your university. Research misconduct can usually be reported in writing to an established research integrity officer in your institution. This is someone trained to follow up and investigate suspected misconduct.

If you suspect research misconduct outside of your university, you can contact the editors or administrators of the journal website where the work appears.

What happens then? Processes are in place in your university to manage the outcomes of research integrity issues. Your research support office can guide you on this.

When research integrity issues or misconduct are identified in published research, these studies are retracted.

The paper is withdrawn from the journal in which it is published. It may still be available online, but it include a notification that it has been retracted.

Retraction Watch, which was launched in 2010, is a blog that tracks retracted papers. The Retraction Watch database contains entries for each retracted paper. By the end of 2020, there were nearly 25,000 retracted papers in the database. Retraction Watch also keeps track of individual researchers with multiple retractions in its leaderboard.

You can check out Retraction Watch here.

How can you demonstrate research integrity?

  • Educate yourself about research integrity.

  • Follow institutional codes of conduct for research. University College Cork‚Äôs research code of conduct is available here.

What can you do in this research to ensure research integrity?

Feedback
While research activities differ across disciplines, there are important things you can do not matter what discipline you are in to ensure that you are ensuring research integrity. Below is a list of some of these activities, you may have thought of others too!

  • You can put into practice the information you have learned in this module.
  • You can consult best practice guidance, like the codes of conduct presented in the additional resources section
  • You can also consult your Research Integrity or Research Support services in your university for support
  • You can be transparent and open at all stages of your research. The other PaPOR TRaIL modules will show you how to engage in open research practices.
  • You should make careful and responsible decisions about what research you will conduct and how you will conduct it
  • You can demonstrate respect for research participants, colleagues, society, culture and the environment
  • You can make sure to always cite all your sources correctly and to ensure you always give credit where it is due when writing up you research
  • You must avoid plagiarism, including self-plagiarism
  • You can make sure you use best practices for research data management- we cover these in the Research Data Management section
  • You must present your data accurately and honestly
  • Never fabricate, manipulate, or misrepresent your data
  • You should make sure to report your research methods and findings honestly and accurately
