What is pre-registration?

Pre-registration is a timestamped record of research processes and decisions. It‚Äôs created in advance, created and shared before data are collected or become accessible and made publicly available. The pre-registration document should be publicly available on a registry or repository.

How is this different to an ethics application? For many research projects, you will need to submit an ethics application. This is typical for studies involving people, existing data, and in the sciences. Pre-registrations and ethics applications contain much of the same information, but they serve different aims.

Why is pre-registration important?

  1. It encourages researchers to identify the goals of their project before collecting data or starting analysis.
  2. It creates accountability in research.
  3. It discourages researchers from conducting after the fact analyses to find significant, novel, or exciting results.
  4. It helps researchers manage the personal biases that might influence their research.
  5. It makes research findings easier to locate.

Why should you pre-register your study?

Pre-registration is easy once you‚Äôve learned how to do it. It builds your reputation as a researcher and allows you to take credit for your ideas and plans. You may also be able to earn a digital badge for pre-registering your study. These are offered by over 60 research journals in multiple disciplines.

Why aren‚Äôt all studies pre-registered?

Pre-registration might present challenges for some types of research like exploratory research or secondary analysis. But challenges do not mean it can‚Äôt be done.

Pre-registration takes time, planning, and figuring out requirements for pre-registration for the target repository.

Some researchers think pre-registration is not applicable or appropriate for more constructivist research. But being transparent about what we plan to do before we begin our research projects is important for any type of research.

How do you pre-register a study?

Pre-registering your study. It sounds like a lot of work. And what if I want to change my research plan?

Your pre-registration is the research plan you will put together with your supervisor and or for ethics approval. So you will be doing much of the work anyway. There are lots of resources and templates you can use to pre-register. This makes it much easier.

You can also update your pre-registration up until you begin data analysis. Your pre-registration will outline how you plan to conduct your study and can be amended up to a point.

Okay, sounds good. So, how do I actually do it?

To pre-register your study, you need to decide the following in advance: your study design and methods, how you will collect your data, and how you will analyze your data.

  1. You should have a clear plan including all the relevant information and details.
  2. You then need to decide where you are going to pre-register your study.
  3. Different pre-registration platforms will provide templates for pre-registering your study.
  4. Templates are meant to serve as a guide because pre-registration may require some flexibility.

Different disciplines may require different methods for pre-registration. A list of discipline specific templates is included in the Additional Resources section. This is not an exhaustive list and there may be other applicable templates.

Pre-registering on OSF

OSF Open Science Framework is a popular repository or platform for pre-registering projects. For this reason, paper trail focuses on OSF for pre-registration.

Pre-registering on OSF

A complete guide to pre-registering projects on OSF is available on their website here. A brief overview is presented on the next slide.

Remember, another platform may be more suitable for you, but for this course, we are just focusing on OSF.

Here are the steps to pre-register on OSF

  1. Set up an account on the Open Science Framework.
  2. Select to start a new pre-registration.
  3. Include your project title and a description. This is called your metadata.
  4. Include your research plan. This is your research design or approach and how you will collect and analyze data.
  5. Register it and make it public. You can make it immediately public or public at a later date.
  6. Preview and review your pre-registration.
  7. You can add attachments if you need more space or have additional components to share.

What is a registered report?

Typically, when we want to publish a paper, we submit it to a journal after the study is finished. The journal and its peer reviewers then decide if the study can be published in that journal.

There is evidence that studies with significant novel or exciting findings are more likely to get published.

What are registered reports?

  1. Registered reports are research papers outlining the proposed study methods and approach.
  2. The potential for publication of these papers is evaluated by the journal before any data collection.
  3. This means they are evaluating the proposed study methods rather than the findings.

What is a Registered Report?

Stage 1.

Researchers submit their research question, questions, theory, hypothesis, detailed methods, analysis plans, and any existing data. Studies are then reviewed. If the review is favorable, the study receives an inprincipal acceptance and is registered in a repository. The research is then conducted.

Stage 2.

Researchers then submit completed manuscripts including:

  1. A justification for the work, and
  2. The approved protocol from stage one and
  3. Results, discussion and any exploratory analysis.

How do you publish a registered report?

  • Plan your study.

  • Submit study proposal to a journal.

  • The proposal will be reviewed.

  • Receive and respond to any feedback, and then if accepted do the study and publish the final paper in the journal.

Why are registered reports important?

  1. They put the focus on the research question and the methodological quality of the study.
  2. They alleviate perceived pressures to obtain significant or exciting results and to publish.
  3. Many journals are starting to adopt registered reports as standard practice.
