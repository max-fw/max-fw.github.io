What is reproducibility?

What is research reproducibility?

  1. It means the research is conducted and reported so that another researcher could reach the same results and conclusions using the same approaches.
  2. It does not mean enabling others to copy research.
  3. It is instead intended to allow people to confirm or correct findings through reproducing the methods.

If another researcher cannot reproduce the research, this does not necessarily mean the findings aren‚Äôt valid. This is because reproducibility does not equal truth. But lack of reproducibility does raise questions about a study‚Äôs validity and reliability.

Reproducibility can also be understood as:

  1. Methods reproducibility. Enough information is provided about the study methods for it to be repeated.
  2. Results reproducibility. An independent study obtains the same results using the original methods.
  3. Inferential reproducibility. Similar conclusions are reached from replicated findings. Reproducible research practices.

Reproducible research involves sharing data and research procedures, making these available for public peer evaluation, taking steps in the study design process to minimize bias.

Reproducibility across disciplines

The concept of reproducibility was developed in science and is primarily oriented around quantitative data. It has traditionally focused on how we can go from a dataset, visualize the information, and demonstrate the analyses that are applied, all of which can be achieved using reproducible scripted workflows.

So reproducibility may reflect scientific assumptions that do not translate easily to other disciplines.

So what about qualitative research, and our research conducted in the social sciences, arts, and the humanities? Exact data replication may not be possible or even advantageous in some cases. Ethical commitments may prevent the sharing of complete datasets.

The amount and kinds of data that can be disclosed in qualitative and social research may depend on a variety of factors: the relationship between researcher and participant, sample characteristics, the research setting, the information being documented, for example, interview data.

There are still approaches that can be taken in non-science disciplines. This type of research should prioritize transparency by providing details about how the research was conducted.

‚ÄúCrisis‚Äù versus ‚ÄúRenaissance‚Äù

There is an argument that what is currently being described as a reproducibility crisis is actually a renaissance in that the focus on reproducibility has highlighted existing problems and proposes ways to address them.

This is resulting in new ways of thinking about and conducting research, and is leading to improvements in research.

Reproducibility examples

To err is to be human.

Often, the mistakes made inadvertently lie hidden, never to be found. Through the incorporation of reproducible practices within our work, we can identify and correct these mistakes after the event, improving the quality of our reporting. Check out the next four slides for examples.

Example 1. Identifying reproducibility issues in a clinical trial.

Clinical trials are studies that examine, typically, treatments on human health outcomes. In 2018, a major medical journal retracted an important clinical trial after a serious error was detected in how the trial data was processed.

In this trial, the treatment and control groups had their coding reversed. This means that what the researchers thought was data from the treatment group was actually data from the control group and vice versa.

This was an inadvertent mistake that invalidated the trial results. The mistake was only found because the code underlying the results was published and so could be examined. Someone who examined the code spotted the mistake. In this way, reproducible research practices saved the day.

Example 2. Power Posing

In 2010, researchers published results from a psychological study suggesting that power posing, or standing or adopting an assertive posture, can improve confidence. These findings were disseminated widely, and the concept of power posing quickly found its way into popular culture.

Two years later, however, the results of the study were called into question when a replication study failed to find the same results. This was particularly the case for changes that the original researchers had reported for certain hormones as a result of power posing.

In addition, increased scrutiny of the original research revealed that the statistical significance values associated with the work were barely significant.

The research was, for a time, dismissed as pseudoscience. However, preregistered and robust replication studies have supported that power posing is linked to people‚Äôs feelings of confidence now. This reaffirms a link between posture and confidence. It also demonstrates the importance of being able to reproduce studies to determine potentially erroneous and/or valid findings.

Why is reproducibility important?

  1. It ensures research can be assessed, verified, and confirmed by other researchers.
  2. It helps identify and correct situations where researchers have engaged in questionable research practices.
  3. Creating a culture of reproducibility can discourage researchers from reporting only those findings that are significant or exciting.

How does this impact you?

Engaging in reproducible practices minimizes the likelihood of questionable research practices, no matter what discipline you are in. It can make research better by reducing bias and/or teasing out other flaws in research. The assessors or markers of your final research project can see how thorough and transparent you have been. While reproducibility practices may take a bit more of your time, they pay off in the end!

If you want to publish your research, journals are now publishing more reproduction studies and negative results.

Being able to assess published research for reproducibility gives you a better understanding of what research is reliable (or not).

It also helps develop a more reliable evidence base for policy or practice decisions that impact society.

How do we make research more reproducible?

What kinds of things decrease reproducibility?

There are lots of things that can impact on reproducibility, things like flaws in study design, researcher biases, and misunderstanding analytical approaches.

Reproducibility may also be decreased by poor record keeping, mistakes, and lack of training, as well as poor sharing of research approaches, selective reporting of findings and methods, and incentives to publish more and faster at the cost of rigor.

How can we make research more reliable and reproducible?

  • Carefully document your methods using a data management plan.

  • Use a uniform filing system with clear folder and filing names.

  • Store metadata alongside the primary data. Metadata is data about data.

  • Store all research materials in accordance with institutional guidelines.

  • Consider keeping a digital notebook to share processes and steps used to generate findings.

How can we make research more reliable and reproducible?

If you are working with data that can be more readily made available, make your data and details of methods freely available to other researchers by publishing the data in a repository such as OSF, which you were introduced to in module two. Always including a data dictionary to accompany your data. A data dictionary gives clear information about your data. Using open-sourced software and providing information about statistical tests or other analytical approaches employed and the arguments used.

In some disciplines, it‚Äôs not as easy or possible to make data available. If it is not possible, remember the importance of enhancing transparency by providing details about how the research was conducted. These details can include the theoretical method adopted, the research setting, the relationship between researcher and participants, anonymized patient characteristics.

Strategies to make your research more reproducible are presented throughout the PaportTrail course: Research Integrity, Research Data Management, Open Reporting, Knowledge Dissemination.

There are resources available to help. Examples of resources in University College Cork include a digital badge in the responsible conduct of research that includes training on research reproducibility.

There are post-graduate modules on reproducible research Practices, PG6030, and an Introduction to Research Integrity, Ethics, and Open Science, PG6015.

The UCC Boole Library also run a variety of training sessions. Other universities may have supports including training and workshops to support you to develop reproducible research methods.

There are also online networks and communities you can follow and join. For example, the UK Reproducibility Network, UKRN.

  • The UKRN provides guidance and resources for methods and strategies for improving research reproducibility.

  • Reproducibility for Everyone, this provides resources and webinars on strategies for reproducible research.

  • ReproducibiliTea, a community for early-stage researchers interested in open science and reproducibility.

What efforts are universities and supervisors making to help us make research more reproducible?

Efforts are increasing by supervisors and universities to support students.

One example of this, being led by Dr. Katherine Button, the University of Bath, is a collaborative approach to open research projects. In this model, students in a number of UK universities participate in a consortium to complete their final-year theses. The students receive extra support throughout the project to make their research reproducible.
