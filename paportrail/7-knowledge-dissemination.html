What is the traditional approach to knowledge dissemination?

What is open knowledge dissemination?

Dissemination is the term used to describe how research is shared. It includes the processes researchers use to share research with other researchers and the general public.

To understand what open dissemination is, it is helpful to first think about how dissemination of research has typically been conducted. Traditional dissemination consisted of the steps outlined in the next slide.

How is research typically disseminated?

Step 1. Designing and conducting research.

Step 2. Analysing data and generating results.

Step 3. Writing up the research findings in the form of a journal article and submitting this for publication in an academic journal.

Step 4. The study undergoes peer review, and a decision is made about whether the article can be accepted, needs to be revised or is rejected.

Step 5. Eventually the article will be published on the Journal website and or in print.

What are some of the challenges of traditional knowledge dissemination?

  • It can take a long time for researchers to get their findings published and for anyone interested to be able to read them.
  • Articles in traditional journals are often behind paywalls, which means they can only be accessed by paying a subscription or fee. This makes it harder for people to access the information, you‚Äôve probably already encountered this as a student.

  • In addition, institutions may subscribe to journals, but not all institutions can afford the same fees. This can create and reinforce inequalities in access to knowledge.

So paywalls and barriers to accessing knowledge also impact on transparency of research. And if research is not disseminated transparently, it can‚Äôt be learnt from and or built upon.

Another problem is that the traditional processes may be biased towards publishing novel, exciting and or significant results. This is a phenomenon known as the file drawer problem. It means that only some findings are available which can bias our understanding and means we only have an incomplete picture.

What are some of the challenges of traditional knowledge dissemination?

It is also a big problem because it can influence which questions researchers choose to ask. So research becomes focussed on what researchers can get published rather than on what we should be examining.

It can also incentivise researchers to engage in questionable research or reporting practises to produce novel, exciting and or significant results.

What is open knowledge dissemination?

  1. It involves sharing research more quickly and openly than in a traditional approach.
  2. It includes approaches that can reduce inequalities by sharing information and broader, more accessible ways.
  3. It is not only open access publishing, although this is an important component.

Methods for openly disseminating research

1. Open access publishing

2. Preprint

3. Online repositories

4. Science communication

5. Citizen science

We will take you through these and the following module sections.

Potential obstacles to open research dissemination

Making research papers openly available involves fees that researchers must pay. This is not always possible.

Some forms of open knowledge dissemination do not require research to be peer reviewed and checked. This may lead to dissemination of lower quality research, however, the challenges of monitoring quality of openly disseminated research may be balanced by increased transparency and post publication peer review. It is also increasingly clear that traditional approaches to dissemination do not also ensure quality either.

Why is open knowledge dissemination important?

Methods for openly disseminating research

Open dissemination methods facilitate:

1. Transparency in research to better access

2. Knowledge for individuals and society

3. Equity in access to knowledge

4. Reshaping of how researchers are rewarded or incentivised to do research

5. Rewarding methodological rigour and integrity instead of just findings

6. Quicker availability of research findings compared to traditional routes

7. Making research more easily accessible with less or no cost.

Open dissemination methods can increase your visibility as a researcher, and the visibility of your research. They make it easier for you to access the research and information you need, and give you more control over what, when and how you share your research.

Open Access Publishing

What is open access publishing?

Open access publishing means publishing research in a format so that it is made openly available for anyone to read without any cost to the reader. There are many dedicated open access journals where people can publish their research openly. There are also increasing numbers of journals offering an open access publishing option in addition to the standard publishing approach. Research funders are increasingly requiring funded researchers to publish their findings open access.

Open access publishing has costs for the researcher, though, who usually has to pay fees to make their research openly available.

This is a barrier to open publishing. It also reflects a bigger problem of costs in publishing, either way, that public money funds research, but there are always costs involved for the public to be able to access.

There are other approaches like preprint that can help get around these issues. We‚Äôll discuss these soon.

Benefits of open access

  • It is easier for everyone, including you, to access research if it is published as open access.

  • Open access publishing allows citizens to share in knowledge and evidence on issues that affects them.

  • Open access publications can increase exposure and impact of your research.

  • Open access publications are reported to receive more media coverage.

  • A lot of research is funded by public money awarded through grants, so open access makes sure the public can see what they have funded.

How do I make my research open access?

If you go on to publish your research project, you can consider publishing it in an open access publication. The directory of open access journals provides a list of such journals across multiple disciplines.

Remember, though, that open access publications have costs for you as the researcher submitting the paper. You can often apply for open access fee waivers, especially for student work that is not supported by funding. Waivers are not always granted, though, so open access publishing may not always be possible due to cost.

Preprints

What are preprints?

Preprints are copies of research manuscripts that have been uploaded to designated preprint servers before undergoing journal peer review. They are the same manuscript that will be submitted to a journal, so they represent the final work.

Are men who smoke at higher risk for more severe case of Covid-19 than women who smoke? A systematic review.

This is an example of a preprint published by students and their supervisor from UCC.

Benefits and drawbacks to preprint

Uploading a pre-print of your manuscript allows for free and rapid dissemination of your research. When reading reprints, it is important to remember that they have not been peer reviewed and so should be read with a degree of caution.

While many journals now have policies allowing and encouraging preconceive research articles, some do not accept preprint. There are ways to check which journals do and don‚Äôt accept preference. We talk more about this shortly.

How do I do a preprint?

If you are planning to publish your research project, you can upload a copy of the final manuscript to a pre-print server. You must do this before you submit it to a journal for peer review.

OK, but how do I actually prepare my manuscript and find where to upload it? An important first step is talking to your supervisor about making your research available as a pre-print.

You should then identify a suitable preprint server. Some examples are archived bioRxiv, medRxiv and arXiv. You can see examples of preference on any of the above servers to help guide you in preparing to upload your preprint.

As your preprint is the same manuscript that you will submit to the journal. Any changes to your manuscript will mostly be formatting and uploading requirements.

It is very important to check that the journal you want to publish your manuscript in accepts prints, share Borromeo provides up to date information about individual journals, open access policies. So check here first.

Once you are sure your target journal accept preprints, you have identified a relevant preprint server and have prepared your paper, you can upload it to the pre-print server.

Repositories

What are repositories?

A repository is a database for storing data sets or research outputs, e.g. manuscript‚Äôs. Many universities have their own repositories for affiliated researchers to use e.g., Coursera and UCC.

Putting research outputs on repositories can make them more visible. Researchers have control over what is made publicly available when putting data or outputs on repositories.

  • Repositories can be domain or discipline specific or maybe more general.
  • Examples of domain specific repositories, Leanness, Health Research, Emelle, a core humanities Izidor, social sciences and humanities.
  • Examples of more general repositories, open science framework and Xanadu.

To upload your research paper, manuscript or data, you must first identify an appropriate repository. Once you have identified a repository, follow the repository guidance to upload your paper or data.

An important note on uploading data to a repository

If you were uploading data to an online repository, make sure you are adhering to ethical considerations and data sharing guidelines.

You should complete the PaporTrail module on Research and Data Management to find out more about this. You should also consult your institutional research and data sharing guidelines and support services.

The OSFI allows researchers to:

1) Store and make available their research outputs

2) manage their workflows and keep projects organised

3) store data analyses and reports

4) assign a digital object identifier to their project

5) link projects to researcher.

Science Communication

What is science communication?

  1. Sharing research ideas, methods and knowledge with non expert audiences.
  2. Sharing these in ways which are useful, understandable, accessible and or tailored.
  3. The goal is to inform the public.
  4. This is usually done in engaging ways to also increase interest in research and its outputs and to influence people‚Äôs opinions and behaviours.

Communicating the findings of your research is as important as conducting the research.

Science communication is not limited to traditional academic approaches, but can incorporate a range of different approaches.

Science communication is not just for science. It can incorporate a range of different approaches aimed at the target audience, including the wider community or special interest groups, e.g. policymaker‚Äôs blogs, public talks and images.

Approaches can include things like public talks, e.g., point of science, social media, including using GIFs, check, hashtag, a bit of cake for good examples of student research.

Blog posts like this one about personalised medicine, videos using visuals or infographics, podcasts like the Orion Open Science Podcast and newspapers and the media.

Benefits of Science Communication

  1. It enhances the sharing of research findings.
  2. It increases public understanding of research.
  3. It increases public interest in research and for it can help research to make an impact by reaching target audiences.

How do I do science communication if you aren‚Äôt ready to share your research with the public?

Yes, a good starting place with science communication is to see how other people are doing is.

  • There are likely blogs relevant to your discipline or research interests that you could look at.

  • Graphical abstracts, which visually represent the research, are becoming increasingly common in journal publications.

  • There are thousands of TED and TED talks, which you can watch Twitter threads, YouTube and other social media include examples of science communication too.

  • There are also local, national and international activities you can attend. Your university may have events and competitions like a three minute thesis where researchers present their research in three minutes.

  • Other activities include things like point of science, where researchers present their research in a pub setting Fame Lab, which is an international science communication competition for researchers, including research students.

When you‚Äôre ready to share your research, you might want to take part in some of these activities.

When you are ready to share your research with the public, it is important to first identify who your target audience is. Is it everyone or is it a specific group that your research is most relevant to?

Think about what message or messages you want to share, what are the key findings, the things that are most important for your audience to know?

Think about ways that you can share this information.

There are lots of options like in-person talks or using videos, visual representations, podcasts or blog posts.

It is important that you avoid using technical terms or jargon when sharing your research with the public, you want it to be accessible to everyone.

Try to include visuals as well as speaking text, this can maintain attention and keep it interesting for the audience.

How do I do science communication?

Make it relatable. Remember, you are not communicating as a researcher to other researchers, so only include examples and anecdotes that are relatable to your target audience.

Remember that because science communication aims for exclusivity, it can be difficult for the audience to decide what is reliable information.

If you were doing science communication, be sure to support your points with accessible data and or supporting information.

If you are viewing science communication, put your researcher hat on to determine if the communicator is supporting their points.

Citizen Science

What is citizen science?

  1. It is an open and inclusive approach to conducting research.
  2. It involves input from the public in the research process as observers identifying images or providing or analysing data.
  3. It is an aim of open research because open research works towards exclusivity,
  4. It is also an enabler of open research because including the public and research increases its openness.
  5. It is not simply having members of the public involved as participants. They are instead active research partners.

Benefits of citizen science

It promotes research that is conducted for the people and by the people. It enhances public engagement in and with research. It nurtures public investment in an understanding of scientific processes and outputs.

Case study: The people‚Äôs trial

Check out the people‚Äôs trial for an excellent example of citizen science and science, communication and action. The public were involved in all stages of the project from deciding what research question to examine, to deciding how the findings should be shared.

For example, the public were asked to come up with possible research questions, they came up with 155 questions.

The public then voted on which one they should examine, the question they decided on was: Does reading a book in bed make a difference to sleep in comparison to not reading a book in bed?

This video was used to explain the what, why and how of deciding a research question.

Find out more about the people‚Äôs trial by visiting their website.

Citizen science is a bit trickier to include in a student project. You can get involved in ongoing citizen science projects that you come across. For instance, projects like the All Ireland Ladybird survey that involves the public in tracking Ireland‚Äôs native Ledebur population using a smartphone app.
