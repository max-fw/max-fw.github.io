4. Data Management

Want to know more about...

Open data and data repositories?

Take a look at this handy guide on selecting a data repository: OpenAIRE Guide for Researchers: How to Select a Data Repository

You can read about some concerns and challenges of using open data repositories here Open data repositories: current risks and opportunities

One researcher’s experience of sharing data? Don’t worry, its not as scary as the name of this blogpost suggests: Open Science is really scary y’all

On the challenges of open data sharing

This article discusses some perceived barriers to open data sharing in psychology (but is relevant across disciplines) Houtkoop, B. L., Chambers, C., Macleod, M., Bishop, D. V., Nichols, T. E., & Wagenmakers, E. J. (2018). Data sharing in psychology: A survey on barriers and preconditions. Advances in methods and practices in psychological science, 1(1), 70-85. https://doi.org/10.1177/2515245917751886

Data management and archiving for qualitative data?

This paper outlines how software like NVivo can be used to manage your qualitative data

You can also check out the Irish Qualitative Data Archive, which provides lots of useful information and resources

Or take a look at the Qualitative Data Repository

This paper outlines some challenges with making some qualitative data open: Chauvette et al. (2019). Open data in qualitative research. International Journal of Qualitative Methods, 18. https://doi.org/10.1177/1609406918823863

This paper presents an examination of repository guidelines for sharing qualitative data Antes et al. (2018). Examining data repository guidelines for qualitative data sharing. Journal of Empirical Research on Human Research Ethics, 13(1), 61-73. https://doi.org/10.1177/1556264617744121

Use of spreadsheets for data management

This is a handy article outlining practical recommendations for organizing spreadsheet data. Broman, K. & Woo, K (2018). Data organization in spreadsheets. The American Statistician, 72(1), 2-10. https://doi.org/10.1080/00031305.2017.1375989

Discipline-specific Research Data Management?

Take a look at the Science Europe Guidance Document: Presenting a Framework for Discipline-specific Research Data Management. This looks like a long document but there is an over-all section, followed by discipline-specific sections that will be of more, or less, relevance to you.

The use of FAIR Principles?

This article clarifies what FAIRness is, and what it is not Mons et al.(2017) ‘Cloudy, Increasingly FAIR; Revisiting the FAIR Data Guiding Principles for the European Open Science Cloud’. Information Services and Use, 49 – 56. https://doi.org/10.3233/ISU-170824

This article discusses the FAIR principles and presents useful examples of their use: Wilkinson et al. The FAIR Guiding Principles for scientific data management and stewardship. Sci Data 3, 160018 (2016). https://doi.org/10.1038/sdata.2016.18

How research data management, FAIR and Open are related to each other?

This article clarifies presents the overlaps and distinctions between these three concepts Higman R, Bangert D and Jones S, “Three camps, one destination: the intersections of research data management, FAIR and Open”, Insights, 2019, 32: 18, 1–9; DOI: http://doi.org/10.1629/uksg.468

Resources for data management in University College Cork

The UCC Research Data Service provides information, including videos and resources.

5. Reproducible Research Practices

Want to know more about...

The reproducibility crisis?

Check out this comic strip about the reproducibility crisis; it focuses on psychology but is relevant across disciplines: Repeat After Me: A Cartoon about Reproducibility in Psychology

This blog gives a nice overview, and includes reference to the power posing study we mentioned earlier: Failure is Moving Science Forward: The replication crisis is a sign that science is working

Take a look at this short video about the ‘reproducibility crisis’. The focus in the video is on science but even if you are not studying science, it presents a useful overview and interesting examples of existing reproducibility issues that are relevant across research disciplines.

Reproducibility in qualitative research and the humanities

This paper gives an overview and practical guidelines for reproducibility in the humanities: Peels, R., Bouter, L. (2018). The possibility and desirability of replication in the humanities. Palgrave Commun 4, 95. https://doi.org/10.1057/s41599-018-0149-x

The paper outlines 12 transparency criteria for qualitative work that can help boost transparency and reproducibility: Aguinis, H. & Solarino, A.M. (2019). Transparency and replicability in qualitative research: The case of interviews with elite informants. Strategic Management Journal, 40(8), 1291-1315. https://doi.org/10.1002/smj.3015

How to make your research more transparent and/or reproducible?

Robert Peng’s 3-part Video Checklist for Reproducible Research can help. There are three parts, about 10 minutes each: 1, 2, and 3

The relationship between registered reports and reproducibility? We introduced registered reports in Module 2 and this video draws a link between registered reports and reproducibility Registered Reports as a tool for improving research transparency and reproducibility

Strategies for minimising normal human biases in research

This paper details one researchers experiences and gives tips on how we can ‘fight our own worst instincts’ in research: Nuzzo, R. (2015). Fooling ourselves. Nature, 526(7572), 182-185. https://www.nature.com/news/how-scientists-fool-themselves-and-how-they-can-stop-1.18517

Software for reproducibility Take a look at this ‘toolkit’, which focuses mainly on computational reproducibility: Perkel, J.M. (2018). A toolkit for data transparency takes shape. Nature, 560(7719), 513-515. https://doi.org/10.1038/d41586-018-05990-5

Large-scale reproducibility evaluations

The links below give an overview of some large-scale projects and let you take a look at some of the reproducibility studies that have been conducted Reproducibility Project: Psychology Many Labs Replication Project Replicability Index

The Power Posing example we introduced earlier

This article gives an overview of the power posing research, including criticisms and support of the findings Power Posing is Back: Amy Cuddy Successfully Refutes Criticism

7. Open Knowledge Dissemination

How to make knowledge dissemination more open

Read this preprint called Scientific Utopia: I. Opening Scientific Communication. The title says ‘science’, but it is relevant across disciplines and some sections expand on what we covered here.

The benefits of sharing research openly

Read this report on ‘How Open Science Helps Researchers Succeed’. Again, this is relevant across disciplines, not just science.

Inclusivity in open research

Take a look a this openly available presentation on why: good open science must be inclusive

The benefits of pre-prints for early career researchers?

Read this paper on the benefits of pre-prints for early career researchers: On the value of preprints: An early career researcher perspective.

What journals support open access? You can check out the Directory of Open Access Journals which is a free online directory of open access journals You can also look at Sherpa Romeo which is an online resource containing information on publisher open access policies

How to visually present your research?

This paper on Ten simple tips for drawing scientific comics gives a great step by step guide (with pictures) for how to draw your research in comic form. Again this is applicable across disciplines, and is a lot of fun!

Or take a look at The Pásalo Project: Dissemination of knowledge via the arts, which is an organisation of professionals from different disciplines who have come together to disseminate information through the arts.
